What is epilepsy?

Source: Cleveland Clinic

Definition

Also called a seizure disorder, epilepsy is a long-term (chronic) neurological disease that affects people of all ages.
This condition is characterized by recurrent and unpredictable seizures due to abnormal activity in the brain. These seizures result from excessive and synchronized discharges of a group of neurons in the brain, temporarily disrupting normal brain functions.
The formal definition of epilepsy includes the presence of two unprovoked seizures occurring more than 24 hours apart.
Epileptic seizures can vary greatly in terms of manifestation and severity, making this disease complex and multifaceted.
Around 50 million people worldwide have epilepsy, making it one of the most common neurological diseases globally, according to the World Health Organization.

Causes

The causes of epilepsy are varied and can be classified into several categories:

  • Genetic Causes: Some forms of epilepsy are inherited and can be attributed to specific mutations in genes involved in neuronal function.
  • Structural Causes: Abnormalities in the brain structure, such as congenital malformations, head trauma, or brain tumors, can provoke seizures.
  • Metabolic Causes: Metabolic imbalances, such as disorders in the metabolism of carbohydrates or lipids, can also be the origin of epilepsy.
  • Infectious Causes: Infections of the central nervous system, like meningitis or encephalitis, can trigger epileptic seizures.
  • Immune Causes: Some epilepsies may be related to immune system dysfunctions, where antibodies attack neurons.

Effects

Epilepsy has significant consequences on the lives of affected individuals, impacting various aspects:

  • Physical Consequences: There is an increased risk of injuries during seizures, as well as side effects related to medication treatments.
  • Psychological Consequences: Depression, anxiety, and mood disorders are frequently observed in epileptic patients.
  • Social Consequences: Stigmatization, discrimination, and social isolation are common, affecting quality of life and interpersonal relationships.
  • Economic Consequences: The costs associated with healthcare, loss of productivity, and inability to work represent a significant economic burden.

Diagnosis

Experiencing more than two seizures without any known medical history or condition such as alcohool withdrawal or low blood sugar can be a sign of epilepsy.

In order to diagnose this condition, possible tests include :

  • Electroencephalography (EEG) to measure the electrical activity in the brain;
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) to look for tumors, infections or blood vessel abnormalities.

But before making a diagnosis, a consultion with a healthcare provider would be necessary to perform a physical examination.
